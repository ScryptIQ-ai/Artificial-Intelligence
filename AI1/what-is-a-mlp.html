    Introduction

    The main objective of this lesson is to obtain an overview of how neural networks work, and to provide you with a solid foundation, should you wish to dive deeper into these models, and apply them in your research.

    We will primarily explore a 'vanilla neural network': which represents the simplest network architecture and is not really used in practice. It is, however, very useful in aiding an understanding the fundamentals of training deep neural networks.

    What is deep learning?

    If you have completed the course up to this point, you should be familiar with classical machine learning as a concept: a collection of techniques that allow computers to improve their predictions by learning from patterns and structure in your data. Classical machine learning approaches will therefore often require humans to carefully decide how data should be represented before learning can take place.

    Deep learning is an extension of this, where this process is largely automated through the use of neural networks with multiple layers, which learn patterns and representations directly from your data. This is the important distinction: instead of relying on carefully selected and provided features, deep learning models automatically discover useful features by progressively transforming raw inputs in an increasingly abstract way.

    This layered learning process makes deep learning particularly effective for complex tasks such as image recognition, speech transcription and natural language comprehension, where explicit rule-based solutions are often very challenging to define. These so-called intuitive problems - tasks that are easy for human cognition to process, but are difficult to describe using explicit rules - are where deep learning excels.

    This strength is thought to come from the combination of:

    • Learning from experience

    • Representing information hierarchically

    Both these characteristics allow deep learning models to capture subtle and abstract patterns in data.

    KEY TERMS

    Broadly speaking, Artificial intelligence (AI) is the field of computer science concerned with building systems that can perform tasks which normally require human intelligence. These tasks include learning from experience, recognising patterns, reasoning under uncertainty, making decisions and adapting behaviour, in response to new information.

    Rather than being explicitly programmed with fixed rules for every situation, AI systems are often designed to identify structure in data, using this to guide their actions. In this sense, 'intelligence' is not hard-coded, but emerges from the interaction between algorithms, data and learning processes.

    HISTORY

    An interesting historical quirk of AI is that, as soon as a task becomes reliable and part of a routine, it often stops being labelled as “AI” at all. This phenomenon is known as the AI effect.

    A good example of this is recognising handwritten characters. Once considered a core AI challenge, it is now so commonplace that it is rarely thought of as intelligent behaviour. We will be using this as our example later in this lesson.

    What are neural networks?

    KEY TERMS

    The term neural network refers to a computational model inspired by the structure and workings of biological nervous systems. It consists of interconnected units called neurons that process information by applying weighted transformations to inputs and passing the results through non-linear activation functions. Training involves adjusting these weights, so that the neural network learns to approximate complex relationships between inputs and outputs, enabling it to model patterns in data and make predictions.

    Neural networks learn by building internal representations of data. These representations are hierarchical in nature: simpler patterns are combined to form more complex concepts. Conceptually, think of neural networks as learning a layered or nested view of the world.

    Many classical machine learning methods depend heavily on how input data is represented. The process of transforming this data into more informative values is known as feature extraction. However, deciding which features are truly relevant is often difficult. One of the key advantages of deep learning is that the network itself learns these features directly from data. As a result, learned representations often outperform hand-crafted ones, and tend to generalise better to new tasks.

    We will begin by examining a single artificial neuron, before building up to a simple neural network known as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). Using this model, we will see how neural networks are trained, evaluated and used to make predictions (inference).

    Should you wish to explore how artificial intelligence, machine learning and deep learning relate to one another in more depth, please head to the Further Reading section.

    Neurons & layers

    Neurons are the basic unit of a neural network, and they are organised in layers. Remember that it is this eventual, cumulative hierarchy of concepts (represented by each layer) that is characteristic of deep learning.

    In the figure below, we illustrate a neural network consisting of:

    • An initial layer of 3 neurons and

    • A second layer with only 1 neuron

    Think of each neuron as holding a number. In the example above:

    • The neurons in the initial layer hold each of the input values: $x_1, x_2, x_3$

    • The single neuron in the second layer holds the output of a certain function $f$, that takes as input the output of all the neurons in the previous layer

    • This function $f$ is called the activation function.

    As we will see later in the lesson, there are different types of activation function.

    KEY TERMS

    An activation function is a mathematical function applied to the output of a neuron that introduces non-linearity into a neural network. It determines whether, and to what extent, a neuron is activated, allowing the network to model complex and non-linear relationships in data.

    What is a Multi-Layer Perceptron?

    Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs)

    A MLP is a direct extension of the aforementioned single-neuron idea, formed by combining many neurons across multiple layers.

    An MLP consists of several distinct types of layers:

    • The first layer is called the input layer

    • The final layer is called the output layer

    • Any layers between the input and output are known as hidden layers, each typically containing many neurons

    Selecting neural network architecture

    A natural question arises at this point: how many neurons should each layer contain, and how many layers should the network have?

    The number of layers and neurons depends strongly on the task at hand and is often chosen empirically, often determined via experimentation, benchmarking and comparisons on similar problems, rather than being directly derived from first principles. This often takes the form of training and comparing multiple architectures to see which performs best on validation data.

    That said, there are also systematic methods for comparing different network architectures and evaluating their performance in a more principled way. One common approach is to treat the architecture itself as a hyperparameter (a setting of the model) that can be optimised through experimentation. This typically involves training multiple networks with different configurations: such as the number of hidden layers, the number of neurons per layer, or both, and comparing their performance. The architecture that achieves the best score, achieving the smallest losses whilst avoiding overfitting is then selected for a final, full training and evaluation.

    ![](images/Fig_architecture.png)

    KEY TERMS

    With reference to neural networks:

    • Width refers to the number of neurons per layer.

    • Depth refers to the number of layers in a network.

    In general, deeper networks (those with more layers) and wider networks (those with more neurons per layer) have greater capacity to learn the underlying relationships within the dataset. However, increased depth or width comes with caveats: more parameters can often translate to longer training times and a greater risk of overfitting (as the model learns meaningless noise unique to the training data, rather than the meaningful structure within it).

    NOTE

    For many practical problems, you often start with as big a network as your computational budget allows, and then use regularisation techniques (such as L2, drop out and input noise) to restrict the effects of overfitting (we will cover this later, in greater detail). Whilst you could start with a smaller network to reduce overfitting, it is often better to approach it with this more 'top down' approach, in order to maximise the capacity of the model to learn the underlying patterns in the data, and obtain the best results possible, given your hardware's capacity.

    You can read more about neural network architectures in our Further Reading section.

    Bias-variance trade-off

    KEY TERMS

    Bias – The systematic error introduced by simplifying assumptions in a model. High bias leads to underfitting and consistently inaccurate predictions on both training and unseen data.

    Variance – The error introduced when a model is overly sensitive to fluctuations in the training data. High variance leads to overfitting and poor generalisation to new data.

    The graph below illustrates what is termed the bias-variance trade-off. As model complexity increases (for example, by adding more layers or neurons), the training error (blue curve) consistently decreases. The model becomes better at fitting the training data.

    However, the validation error (red curve) behaves differently. It initially decreases, as the model captures meaningful structure in the data. Beyond a certain point, though, it begins to increase. This happens because the model starts to fit noise and idiosyncrasies in the training set, rather than any meaningful structure. This is referred to as overfitting.

    Underfitting (left side): The model is too simple.

    • High training error

    • High validation error

    • High bias, low variance
    Overfitting (right side): The model is too complex.
    • Very low training error

    • Increasing validation error

    • Low bias, high variance
    The optimal model lies at the minimum of the validation error curve, where bias and variance are balanced. The goal in machine learning is not to minimise training error alone, but to find this balance so the model generalises well to new data.

    How a MLP functions

    Within a MLP, neurons behave in the same way as in the simpler single-neuron case. At each layer after the input layer, every neuron:

    • Receives as input the outputs of all neurons in the previous layer,

    • Applies an activation function $f$ to these inputs, and

    • Produces a single output value.
    This process is repeated layer by layer until the output layer is reached.

    NOTE

    Because every neuron in one layer is connected to every neuron in the previous layer, MLPs are often referred to as fully-connected networks. Other types of connectivity exist, and we will encounter them later when studying convolutional neural networks in Artificial Intelligence 2.

    Input and output neurons

    KEY TERMS

    Input neurons – these are nodes in the input layer that receive the raw features of the dataset and pass them into the network.

    Intermediate (hidden) neurons – nodes in the hidden layers that transform the input through weighted connections and activation functions to extract patterns.

    Output neurons – nodes in the output layer that produce the model’s final prediction or classification.

    The neurons in the input and output layers of a neural network are slightly unique, relative to those in hidden layers. Their values are not produced by a standard hidden-layer activation functions in the same way as intermediate neurons.

    This is easiest to understand through a concrete example. Suppose we use an MLP to classify an image of a handwritten digit into an actual, labelled digit from 0 to 9. Each image has $784$ pixels, which are $784$ different points of information or features in machine learning terms.

    The input layer

    The neurons in the input layer simply store the input data. So for these images this means 784 neurons, each holding the value of a single pixel.

    If we were to gain new images that were of a higher resolution (more pixels), we would need to create a new network with more neurons in the input layer, or downscale the images to match.

    The output layer

    The neurons in the output layer represent the model’s prediction. In this case, the question being answered is: Which digit does this image represent? As we have the digits from 0 to 9, we need 10 output neurons, each representing the probability that the image belongs to a given class.

    Since there are 10 possible digits, a convenient output representation is to use 10 neurons, one per digit. Each neuron holds a value indicating how confident the network is that the input image belongs to that particular class.

    In the next example, we will zoom in into what actually goes on in a single neuron computation.

    Computing the neuron output

    A neuron in a hidden layer performs the following sequence of operations:

    • It receives as input the outputs $x$ from all neurons in the previous layer.

    • It computes a weighted sum of these inputs $\sum w_i x_i$ and adds a bias term $b$.

    • This becomes $\sum w_i x_i + b$

    A schematic representation of this process is shown in this diagram:

    The need for non-linearity

    Looking at this equation, it is clear that the output of a neuron is a linear combination of its inputs. If a neural network was left with just this linear combination, it would be no better than a linear regression model. We can quickly visualise why this is the case with some Python code to demonstrate that no matter how many layers we stack, composing linear transformations only produces another linear transformation.

    # Lets first import matplotlib and numpy
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

    Below is a simple 3-layer network where each layer is just a linear transformation, as defined by three sets of weights and biases. Taking a range of input values $x$ and passing them sequentially through each linear transformation (left plot), as would happen in a neural network, produces an output that is mathematically equivalent to a single linear transformation (right plot).

    # Define three sequential linear transformations (representing 3 layers)
w1, b1 = 2.0, 1.0    # First layer weights and bias
w2, b2 = 0.5, -0.5   # Second layer weights and bias  
w3, b3 = 3.0, 2.0    # Third layer weights and bias

# Generate input values
x = np.arange(-3, 3, 0.1)

# Compute the output after passing through all three layers
layer1_output = w1 * x + b1
layer2_output = w2 * layer1_output + b2
layer3_output = w3 * layer2_output + b3

# Now compute what a single equivalent linear transformation would be
# Mathematically: w3(w2(w1*x + b1) + b2) + b3 = (w3*w2*w1)*x + (w3*w2*b1 + w3*b2 + b3)
equivalent_w = w3 * w2 * w1
equivalent_b = w3 * w2 * b1 + w3 * b2 + b3
single_layer_output = equivalent_w * x + equivalent_b

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 4))

# Left plot: the three-layer computation
ax1.plot(x, layer3_output, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='3-layer network')
ax1.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
ax1.axvline(x=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
ax1.set_xlabel('Input x')
ax1.set_ylabel('Output y')
ax1.set_title('Three Linear Layers')
ax1.legend()
ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Right plot: the equivalent single layer
ax2.plot(x, single_layer_output, 'r--', linewidth=2, label='Equivalent single layer')
ax2.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
ax2.axvline(x=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
ax2.set_xlabel('Input x')
ax2.set_ylabel('Output y')
ax2.set_title('Single Equivalent Layer')
ax2.legend()
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

fig.suptitle('Stacking Linear Transformations: Depth Adds No Expressiveness', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

    We therefore need to pass the result of this computation, $\sum w_i x_i + b$, through a non-linear activation function, which is labelled as $h$ in the previous schematic.

    The non-linearity of the activation function is a crucial ingredient. Without non-linear activation functions, even very deep networks would collapse into simple linear models. It is this non-linearity that allows an MLP - given sufficient neurons - to approximate highly complex functions.

    The activation function can be viewed as a mathematical model of how an artificial neuron responds to its input. It is loosely inspired by biological neurons, which only activate once their input exceeds a certain threshold. There are many possible choices for non-linear activation functions and one of the most commonly used is the rectified linear unit (ReLU).

    Activation Functions

    The ReLU function

    With a ReLU activation, the output $h$ of a neuron is computed as:

    $$h = \max(0, wx + b)$$

    Here, $x$ denotes the input data, $w$ the neuron's weights, and $b$ the bias term. The weighted sum $wx + b$ can be interpreted as the neuron attending to different components of the input (via the weights), whilst the bias term controls how strong the combined input must be before the neuron becomes active.

    The ReLU function returns either 0 or the value $wx + b$, depending on whether $wx + b$ is negative or positive. In essence, this neuron will ignore (zero out) the input if the weighted sum plus bias is zero or less, but if greater than zero the information is retained and passed through linearly.

    Visualising the above equation across a range of inputs demonstrates this behaviour nicely.

    # A visual demonstration of the ReLU function

# We define the relu function using numpy's maximum 
def relu(x):
    return np.maximum(0,x) # Returns whichever value is greater of 0 and x

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(12, 4))

# Define the input values as a range with Numpy's arange function:
x = np.arange(-5, 5, 0.1) # The values are given as start, stop and step
x_pos_mask = x > 0 # Create a filter for only values above 0

y = relu(x) # call our function
ax.plot(x[x_pos_mask], y[x_pos_mask], linewidth=8, c="g") # plot x > 0 values
ax.plot(x[~x_pos_mask], y[~x_pos_mask], linewidth=8, c="r") # plot x <= 0 values
ax.set_title("ReLU")
ax.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.5)
ax.axvline(x=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.5)
ax.set(xlabel='Input x', ylabel='Output y')


    [Text(0.5, 0, 'Input x'), Text(0, 0.5, 'Output y')]

    Notice how the ReLU creates a sharp threshold at zero. Any negative input is completely silenced, whilst positive inputs pass through unchanged. This simple non-linearity is an example of what allows neural networks to learn complex, non-linear relationships in data. Without it, stacking multiple layers would be mathematically equivalent to a single linear transformation, severely limiting what the network could represent.

    ReLU is widely used because of its favourable properties during optimisation with gradient descent. However, it is not the only possible choice.

    KEY TERMS

    Gradient descent is an optimisation algorithm used to minimise a function by iteratively adjusting its parameters in the direction of steepest decrease. In the context of neural networks, it updates the model’s parameters by moving them in the opposite direction of the gradient of the loss function, gradually reducing prediction error.

    A loss function is a mathematical function that quantifies how far a model’s predictions are from the true target values. It provides a numerical measure of error, which is used during training to guide optimisation, improving the model’s performance.

    We will explore both of these in the next sub-module, Training a MLP.

    Other activation functions

    Beyond ReLU, two other common activation functions are worth exploring: the hyperbolic tangent (also known as tanh) and the sigmoid function.

    For tanh, the neuron's output is given by:

    $$h = \tanh(wx+b)$$

    For sigmoid, the neuron's output is given by:

    $$\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}$$

    where $z = wx+b$.

    As before, $x$ represents the input vector, $w$ the weights, and $b$ the bias term.

    Unlike ReLU, both tanh and sigmoid are smooth, S-shaped functions that squash their inputs into bounded ranges. The tanh function outputs values between -1 and +1, whilst the sigmoid function outputs values between 0 and 1.

    The code cell below visualises and compare the behaviour of these three activation functions: tanh, sigmoid, and ReLU.

    # There are multiple activation functions that can be used for a neuron. 
# Instead of just relu, we can also use sigmoid, tanh, among others.

def sigmoid(x):
    return 1/(1 + np.exp(-x))

def relu(x):
    return np.maximum(0,x)


# Define the sigmoid, ReLU, and tanh activation functions:
activation_functions = [np.tanh, sigmoid, relu]

# Plot the activation functions as separate plots:
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(12, 4))

# Define the input values as a range with Numpy's arange function. The values are given as start, stop and step:
x = np.arange(-5, 5, 0.1)

# Loop over the activation functions:
for i, ax in enumerate(axes.flat):
    y = activation_functions[i](x)
    ax.plot(x, y)
    ax.set_title(f'{activation_functions[i].__name__}')
    ax.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
    ax.axvline(x=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
    ax.set(xlabel='Input x', ylabel='Output y')

# Add a sup title:
fig.suptitle('Activation Functions', fontsize=16)

# Insert a gap between the suptitle and the three plots:
fig.subplots_adjust(top=0.85)
fig.subplots_adjust(wspace=0.25)
plt.show()

    These graphs show the relationship between the input and the output of each activation function. Notice how ReLU exhibits the sharp thresholding behaviour we discussed earlier, whilst tanh and sigmoid provide smooth, S-shaped transitions.

    All three functions share a common feature: they transform their inputs in ways that create non-linearity. However, their behaviours differ significantly. ReLU passes positive values through unchanged and zeros out negatives. Tanh outputs range from -1 to +1, centred around zero. Sigmoid squashes all inputs into the range 0 to 1, making it particularly useful when interpreting outputs as probabilities.

    Neuron output with activation functions

    In this section, we will define a neuron as a Python function that computes the weighted sum of the inputs, adds to it a bias term, applies an activation function and returns an output. In this case, we will focus on the tanh activation function.

    # using numpy, define a function that serves the role of an individual neuron:
def neuron(x, w, b, activation_function=np.tanh):
    # calculate the dot product of the inputs and the weights
    z = np.dot(x, w) + b
    # apply the activation function
    a = activation_function(z)
    return a

    Now that we have defined the function of a single neuron, we can experiment with different weight values, to observe their effect on the neuron's output. For clarity, we will set the bias term to zero, that is $b=0$. And, using a for loop, we can specify a specific range of input weights $w$ to display.

    # To see the effect of different weights for a given input, we can plot the output of the neuron for different weights.
# Create a numpy array, and specify values from 
for i in np.arange(-0,1.1,0.2):  # a range of weights from 0.0 to 1.0, in steps of 0.2
    x = np.arange(-4, 4, 0.1)
    y = neuron(x, w=i, b=0)
    plt.plot(x, y, label=f'w = {i.round(2)}')

    # Within matplotlib.pyplot, we can specify a legend, its position and x- and y-axis labels, as follows:
    plt.legend(bbox_to_anchor=(1.05, 1), loc='upper left', borderaxespad=0.)
    plt.xlabel('Input x')
    plt.ylabel('Output y')
    
# Specify dotted lines at x=0 and y=0 for reference
plt.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)
plt.axvline(x=0, color='k', linestyle='--', lw=0.2)

plt.title('Neuron with different input weights, and no bias')

plt.show()

    Notice how the shape of the curve changes with different weight values. For large values of $x$, the larger the weight $w$, the larger the output $y$. Additionally, for a given increase in input $x$, a curve with a larger weight $w$ produces a much larger increase in output $y$. That is, the ramp up towards large output values is sharper for a larger weight.

    These two examples showcase how the weights and bias of a neuron change its response to inputs. By changing the weights, we can modify how much positive or negative input values are amplified or suppressed. By changing the bias term, we can change the threshold value from which the neuron will return a positive value.

    What the outputs "mean"

    Throughout this lesson, we have been building up an understanding of what happens inside a neural network. It is worth pausing, however, to ask a practical question: once an input has been propagated through all layers and their associated weights, biases, and activation functions, what does the output actually represent?

    The answer depends on the task. The two most common cases are classification and regression.

    In a classification task, the network assigns an input to one of several discrete categories. In the handwritten digit example, there are ten possible classes: the digits 0 to 9. The output layer therefore contains ten neurons, one per class. After the forward pass, each neuron holds a raw numerical score, reflecting how strongly the network associates the input with that class. The class with the highest score (positive) is taken as the model's prediction, those with ever smaller or negative scores are progressively less likely according to the model. These are typically converted into a probability distribution, which we will cover in the next section.

    In a regression task, the goal is to predict a continuous value rather than a discrete label. Here, the output layer typically contains a single neuron, and its raw output is taken directly as the prediction. For example, a temperature, a concentration, or a physical measurement. No further transformation is needed.

    When to use each activation function

    The choice of activation function can impact your network's performance and training behaviour. Typically ReLU will be the default choice for most hidden layers in modern neural networks. Its simplicity makes it computationally efficient. However, ReLU neurons can sometimes "die" during training: if a neuron's output becomes zero for all training examples, it will stop learning entirely, as its gradient will always be zero. To counter this, in deep neural networks data scientists will often use the ReLU variant - Leaky ReLU - which doesn't reduce certain inputs to zero, rather it makes then very small by a factor of alpha.

    Tanh can be useful when you need outputs centred around zero. It was more commonly used in older network designs but has largely been supplanted by ReLU for hidden layers. Tanh outputs are bounded between -1 and +1, which can provide stability in some contexts.

    Sigmoid is rarely used in hidden layers of deep networks due to severe vanishing gradient problems, when the gradient becomes very small for extreme input values. However, sigmoid remains valuable for the output layer in binary classification tasks, where you need to produce a probability between 0 and 1. Similarly, for multi-class classification, a related function called softmax (next secion) is used to produce a probability distribution over multiple classes.

    In practice, unless you have a specific reason to do otherwise, starting with ReLU for hidden layers is a sensible default.

    Summary

    In this lesson, we explored the fundamental architecture of neural networks, focusing on the MLP as a foundational model for understanding deep learning. We examined how neurons—the basic computational units—are organised into layers (input, hidden, and output) and how each neuron computes its output by taking a weighted sum of its inputs, adding a bias term, and applying a non-linear activation function. Activation functions include ReLU, tanh, and sigmoid, as well as others, all with differing behaviours that can guide a model's output.
